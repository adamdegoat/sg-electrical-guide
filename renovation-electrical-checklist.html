Renovation electrical checklist

⚡ Evergrow Electrical Service Reviewed by a Licensed Electrical Worker

The single most expensive electrical mistake in a reno is realising you need a socket or a light after the tiles and carpentry are in. Sort the electrical plan early, and most of these become cheap. Here's what to think through.

Do the electrical work first

Wiring, points and concealed runs happen before tiling, painting and carpentry — chasing a wall open after it's finished means re-doing the finishing too. Lock your electrical plan in at the start with your contractor and electrician.

Points — plan room by room

  • Kitchen: the heavy hitter. Fridge, oven, hob, microwave, kettle, airfryer — these often need their own dedicated circuits, not all shared on one.
  • Living & TV console: more sockets than you think (TV, console, soundbar, router, chargers).
  • Bedrooms: bedside points each side, a study-desk cluster, and a dedicated aircon point.
  • Bathrooms / service yard: water heater, washer/dryer.

The bigger-ticket decisions

  • Lighting plan: downlights, cove lighting, feature pendants — and where the switches go (and whether you want them dimmable or smart).
  • DB box: adding aircon, a water heater and a kitchen-load often means the old board needs upgrading — cheaper to do it during the reno.
  • Data & network points: run network cabling now if you want wired internet or wall-mounted Wi-Fi.
  • Aircon points: coordinate with your aircon installer so the power is where the units land.
✓ Finish the job right

However you split the work between your ID firm and electrician, make sure the electrical is done by an EMA-licensed worker and that the circuits are tested and certified at the end. That's your proof it was done properly.

Our honest advice: walk each room with your electrician and picture a normal day — where you'll stand, charge, cook, work. Adding two extra points now costs a fraction of calling someone back to one finished wall later.
